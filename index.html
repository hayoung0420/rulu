Importance of pronouncation

It is never necessary to pronounce it the same as a native pronunciation. However, if you do not know the exact pronunciation, communication problems arise. Let's recognize the importance of pronunciation by looking at the example situations below.

The situation at work

Situation: A Japanese employee asks for important documents from the team leader, but they are preparing "pies" with a
leader: Could you prepare all the files for the meeting?
staff: (pronunciation mistake) Oh, sure! I will get all the pies ready.
leader: (embarrassment) What? Pies? No, I said files, not pies!
Description: Japanese doesn't have [f] pronunciation, so it can be pronounced [p]. This can cause confusion at work

The situation at school

Situation: A Japanese student asks his teacher "third question," but mispronounces it as "first question."
student: Can you help me with the third question?
Why do you want to help with that?
student: No, no! I mean the third question!
Description: [th] Pronunciation incorrectly as [s] resulting in a situation where the content of the question cannot be conveyed properly.

Daily situation

Situation: A Japanese friend tries to show off "knit," but mispronounces it and is mistaken for "lipstick."
A friend: I just got a new knit sweater!
B friend: Oh, you think lipstick?
A friend: No, I said knit sweater, not lipstick!
Description: [n] I mispronounce the pronunciation [l], which changes what my friend asked.

Why is Japanese and Korean different?

This is something you need to know before you pronounce it!

Opened syllables vs Closed syllables

Discontinuation & Lung Syllable? Simply put, an opening syllable is a word that a word can end in a vowel, and a closing syllable ends in a consonant! Korean and English have both opening and closing syllable structures, but Japanese only has an opening syllable structure, so even the original words that end in consonants must be forced to end in vowels, so even the same English word can sound awkward to us!
 EX Open syllables: 'ga', 'na', 'go', etc. vs. closed syllables: 'home', 'cat', 'top', etc. -> Japanese end up with a consonant or awkwardly pronounce words with consonant support [McDonald's is pronounced as 'MacDonald' by Koreans, and 'macudonardo' because Japanese cannot pronounce consonant support!]!

Japanese English?

Have you ever heard of Konglish? Konglish is a combination of Korean and English, and it is an English expression that has been transformed into Korean. Likewise, Japanese is a combination of Japanese and English, and when these expressions are used in foreign countries, foreigners often don't understand. Not only do foreigners not know what the word itself is, but Japanese pronunciation of the word itself is difficult to understand.
 EX 'dress shirt' That's the correct English expression for shirts that we all know. However, what Japan used to call a white shirt because of its white color became common in modern dress shirts.

In Japan, English education and learning are the only grammar?

English education in the Japanese educational field has been mainly based on grammar from the past (1970~80) and teaching methods of improving cognitive ability and improving understanding of language through the process of translating the target language into one's own mother tongue, so there is not a well-established system to educate students on English pronunciation, so students did not have the opportunity to experience proper English pronunciation in the first place!
